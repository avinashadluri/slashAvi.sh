> _Gear doesn't make the photographer. But the right gear removes friction between your eye and the image._

I researched cameras for months before buying one. Watched every comparison video, read every forum thread, agonized over specs. In hindsight, I should have just bought anything and started shooting sooner. But the engineer in me needed to understand the system first.

Here's what I ended up with and why.

---

## The Camera

| Spec | Details |
|---|---|
| **Body** | Sony A6400 |
| **Type** | APS-C mirrorless |
| **Sensor** | 24.2 MP APS-C Exmor CMOS |
| **Crop factor** | 1.5× |
| **Autofocus** | 425-point phase detection + 425-point contrast detection |
| **Eye AF** | Real-time Eye AF (human + animal) |
| **Video** | 4K 30fps, 1080p 120fps |
| **ISO range** | 100–32,000 (expandable to 102,400) |
| **Weight** | 403g (body only) |

### Why the A6400?

I considered the A7 III (full-frame), the Fuji X-T4, and the Canon M50 II. The A6400 won because:

- **Autofocus is unreal.** The real-time eye tracking locks on and doesn't let go. For someone still learning composition, not having to worry about focus is a gift.
- **Compact and light.** I carry it daily. A full-frame body would have stayed home more often.
- **APS-C is a feature, not a compromise.** The 1.5× crop gives me extra reach for wildlife and birds — my 70-350mm effectively becomes 105-525mm.
- **Price-to-capability ratio.** It punches way above its price, especially on the used market.
- **Sony lens ecosystem.** The E-mount lens library is massive. I can start with affordable APS-C lenses and graduate to full-frame glass when I'm ready.

<Tip>
If you're starting out, don't let anyone tell you APS-C isn't "real photography." The A6400's sensor handles low light, dynamic range, and detail beautifully. The best camera is the one you actually carry.
</Tip>

## Lenses

### Sony E 16-50mm f/3.5-5.6 OSS (Kit Lens)

| Spec | Details |
|---|---|
| **Focal length** | 16-50mm (24-75mm equivalent) |
| **Aperture** | f/3.5-5.6 |
| **Type** | Retractable zoom, pancake-style |
| **Best for** | Everyday carry, travel, street |

The kit lens gets a bad reputation, but it's genuinely useful. It's tiny, covers the most common focal range, and has optical stabilization. I keep it on the camera when I want to travel light. It taught me to work with limitations — you can't blur backgrounds much at f/5.6, so you learn to compose differently.

### Sony E 70-350mm f/4.5-6.3 G OSS

| Spec | Details |
|---|---|
| **Focal length** | 70-350mm (105-525mm equivalent) |
| **Aperture** | f/4.5-6.3 |
| **Type** | Super-telephoto zoom |
| **Best for** | Wildlife, birds, sports, events |

This lens changed everything for me. The reach is extraordinary for an APS-C system — 525mm equivalent lets me photograph birds and wildlife without disturbing them. The G-series build quality is solid, and the optical stabilization is effective enough for handheld shooting. Combined with the A6400's eye AF, it tracks birds in flight remarkably well.

### Sony E 50mm f/1.8 OSS

| Spec | Details |
|---|---|
| **Focal length** | 50mm (75mm equivalent) |
| **Aperture** | f/1.8 |
| **Type** | Portrait prime |
| **Best for** | Portraits, low light, bokeh |

My first prime lens. Shooting at f/1.8 after using kit zoom lenses felt like seeing for the first time. The subject separation, the creamy bokeh, the low-light capability — it fundamentally changed how I think about depth and isolation in a frame. The 75mm equivalent focal length is perfect for candid portraits of the kids.

### Sony E 11mm f/1.8

| Spec | Details |
|---|---|
| **Focal length** | 11mm (16.5mm equivalent) |
| **Aperture** | f/1.8 |
| **Type** | Ultra-wide prime |
| **Best for** | Landscapes, architecture, astrophotography, vlogging |

Ultra-wide at f/1.8 — this lens is special. I use it for Sydney cityscapes, interior architecture, and starry skies. The wide angle forces a completely different way of seeing. You can't just point and shoot — you have to think about foreground interest, leading lines, and depth in a way that tighter lenses don't demand.
