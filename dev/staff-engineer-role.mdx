Every few months someone asks me what I actually do all day. The question usually comes from a senior engineer who's been heads-down shipping features, looks up, and wonders what happens after senior. The honest answer is: it depends on the week, and it's rarely what you'd expect.

When I made the jump to staff, I thought I'd be writing the hardest code, designing the most complex systems, and generally being the best engineer in the room. I was wrong about all three. The role is less about being the best individual contributor and more about making everyone around you better. It's a force multiplier job, and that shift is genuinely disorienting the first time you experience it.

## The Typical Week

Here's what a real week looks like for me. Not the idealized version — the actual one.

| Day | Morning | Afternoon |
|-----|---------|-----------|
| Monday | Technical strategy review, update architecture roadmap | RFC review for two teams, async feedback on design docs |
| Tuesday | Deep coding block (migration tooling) | 1:1s with two engineers I mentor, team sync |
| Wednesday | Cross-team alignment meeting (API contracts) | Prototype for proposed caching approach |
| Thursday | Write strategy doc for Q3 platform direction | Code reviews (6-8 PRs), incident review |
| Friday | Open source contributions, learning block | Ad-hoc pairing sessions, write weekly update |

If you're counting, actual coding is maybe two to three blocks out of ten. That's not because coding doesn't matter — it absolutely does — but because my highest-leverage work happens in those other seven blocks.

<Info>
The ratio shifts depending on the company stage. At Weel (startup), I coded more like 50% of the time because the team was smaller and every hand mattered. At Atlassian (thousands of engineers), it was closer to 20% because the coordination and strategy surface area was massive.
</Info>

## From Best IC to Force Multiplier

The hardest part of becoming a staff engineer is accepting that your individual output matters less than your organizational output. At senior level, you're rewarded for shipping great code fast. At staff level, you're rewarded for making entire teams ship great code faster.

This means your metrics change. Instead of "how many features did I ship?" the questions become:

- How many architectural decisions did I unblock?
- How many engineers leveled up because of my mentorship?
- How many cross-team conflicts did I resolve before they became crises?
- How many bad technical bets did I help the org avoid?

None of these show up in a git log. That's uncomfortable if your identity is tied to your commit history.

## Owning Technical Direction Without Being a Manager

Staff engineers sit in a weird organizational limbo. You own technical direction but nobody reports to you. You're responsible for system-level outcomes but you can't tell anyone what to do. You have to influence through persuasion, trust, and the quality of your ideas.

At Atlassian, I owned the frontend platform strategy for a product area with six teams. That meant I decided (well, "decided" — more like "built consensus around") which frameworks we adopted, how we structured shared libraries, and what our migration path looked like. But if a team disagreed, I couldn't mandate anything. I had to make the case so compelling that teams chose to follow it.

This is simultaneously the most rewarding and most frustrating aspect of the job. When it works, you see technical direction emerge organically across teams because people genuinely believe it's the right approach. When it doesn't work, you watch teams make decisions you know will cause pain, and you have to decide whether to escalate or let it play out.

## Writing as a Core Competency

I write more documents than code at this point, and I'm not exaggerating. RFCs, strategy docs, architecture decision records, one-pagers for leadership, post-incident reviews, onboarding guides. The written word is how staff engineers scale their influence beyond the meetings they attend.

A well-written RFC saves dozens of hours of meetings. A clear strategy document prevents months of misaligned work. A good architecture decision record means you don't have to re-explain the same trade-off to every new hire for the next two years.

<Tip>
If you're aspiring to staff, start writing now. Write design docs for your features even if your team doesn't require them. Write post-mortems that capture real lessons. Write proposals for problems you see. Your writing is your portfolio at this level.
</Tip>

## Navigating Org Politics

Nobody tells you about this part. Staff engineers operate at the intersection of technology and organization, and organizations are political by nature. I don't mean backstabbing and scheming — I mean navigating competing priorities, managing stakeholder expectations, and understanding that technical decisions are always made within an organizational context.

At Weel, the politics were simpler because the team was small. A conversation with the CTO and the product lead could align the entire company's technical direction. At Atlassian, the same alignment required months of relationship-building, multiple rounds of feedback, executive sponsorship, and careful sequencing of proposals to build momentum.

Understanding how decisions actually get made in your organization is a staff-level skill. Who are the real decision-makers? What do they care about? What's the funding model? What are the political dynamics between teams? If you can't answer these questions, your brilliant technical proposal will die in a committee review.

## The Loneliness of the Role

Here's something I wish someone had told me earlier: staff engineering can be lonely. You're no longer purely part of the engineering team's daily rhythm. You sit in meetings with directors and VPs where you're the only IC. You think about problems on a 6-12 month horizon while your team thinks week-to-week. Your peers are other staff engineers scattered across the org, and you might only interact with them in monthly forums.

At Atlassian, I found this particularly acute. The engineers on my team respected me but saw me as somewhat separate — I was the person who showed up to their standups sometimes but spent most of my day in "mysterious meetings." The managers included me in leadership discussions but I wasn't a manager. I existed in an organizational gap.

<Warning>
If you don't actively maintain your technical skills, the gap between you and the codebase will widen fast. I block at least two half-days per week for hands-on coding. It keeps me credible, keeps me grounded, and honestly — it keeps me sane.
</Warning>

## Staying Technical While Going Broad

The breadth-vs-depth tension is real and ongoing. You need to understand enough about twelve different systems to make good cross-cutting decisions, but you also need to go deep enough on at least a few of them to maintain credibility and spot real issues in reviews.

My approach: I maintain deep expertise in two or three areas (frontend architecture, API design, developer tooling) and cultivate "dangerous enough" knowledge in everything else (databases, infrastructure, security, mobile). I pair with specialists when I need to go deeper on something outside my core areas.

The key is being honest about what you don't know. Staff engineers who pretend to be experts in everything lose trust fast. Staff engineers who say "I don't know enough about this — let me bring in someone who does" build trust just as fast.

## Is It Worth It?

Honestly? Some days I miss the simple satisfaction of writing a function, watching tests go green, and shipping a feature. There's a clarity to individual contribution that gets muddied at the staff level.

But then I see an engineer I mentored get promoted. Or I watch a cross-team migration go smoothly because we spent three months building consensus on the approach. Or I read a design doc from a team I've never worked with directly, and it follows the patterns I championed — not because I told them to, but because those patterns proved themselves.

That's the payoff. It's slower, less visible, and harder to measure. But the blast radius of your impact is genuinely different. And for me, that trade-off is absolutely worth it.
