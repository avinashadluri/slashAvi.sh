At 2:47 AM on a Tuesday, I got paged. Payment processing was down at Weel. Real money, real customers, real consequences. The next 15 minutes determined whether this was a 20-minute incident or a 4-hour disaster.

Those first 15 minutes are the most important minutes in any incident. Not because that's when you fix things — it's when you set the trajectory. Good first 15 minutes: clear ownership, fast triage, calm communication. Bad first 15 minutes: confusion, panic, three engineers debugging the same thing while nobody talks to the customer-facing team.

Here's the playbook I've built across incidents at Atlassian (where incidents are a weekly occurrence at scale) and Weel (where every incident is existential because we handle financial transactions).

## The Timeline: What Happens When

| Time | Action | Owner |
|------|--------|-------|
| T+0 | Alert fires, on-call acknowledges | On-call engineer |
| T+2 min | Severity assessment (SEV1-4) | On-call engineer |
| T+3 min | Incident channel created, roles assigned | On-call engineer |
| T+5 min | First status update to stakeholders | Incident communicator |
| T+10 min | Initial hypothesis formed, first mitigation attempted | Resolver(s) |
| T+15 min | Escalation decision — do we need more people? | Incident commander |
| T+30 min | Second status update, refined diagnosis | Communicator |
| T+60 min | Hourly updates until resolution | Communicator |
| Resolution | Final update, incident channel archived | Commander |
| T+48 hrs | Postmortem written and reviewed | All participants |

This timeline isn't aspirational — it's practiced. We run incident drills quarterly at Weel where we simulate failures and practice this exact flow. The first time you follow the playbook shouldn't be during a real incident.

## Severity Levels

Get this wrong and you either wake up the CTO for a broken tooltip or let a payments outage simmer while one engineer casually investigates.

| Severity | Description | Response | Example |
|----------|-------------|----------|---------|
| SEV1 | Customer-facing outage, data loss risk, financial impact | All hands, war room, exec communication | Payment processing down |
| SEV2 | Major feature degraded, affecting many users | On-call + team lead, regular status updates | Search returning stale results |
| SEV3 | Minor feature broken, workaround exists | On-call investigates during business hours | CSV export timing out for large datasets |
| SEV4 | Cosmetic issue, no functional impact | Ticket created, fixed in normal sprint | Button misaligned on settings page |

<Warning>
When in doubt, escalate up. A SEV2 that gets upgraded to SEV1 after 30 minutes wastes 30 minutes. A SEV1 that gets downgraded to SEV2 after 5 minutes wastes 5 minutes. Always err on the side of higher severity.
</Warning>

## The Three Roles

Every incident needs exactly three roles. Not five, not "everyone jump in" — three.

**Incident Commander (IC):** Owns the incident. Makes decisions about escalation, mitigation strategy, and when to declare resolution. Doesn't debug — coordinates. At Weel, the on-call engineer is the IC by default but can hand off if they need to be the resolver.

**Communicator:** Writes status updates. Handles Slack messages to stakeholders, updates the status page, and fields questions from non-engineering teams. This role exists so the resolver can focus on fixing things without being interrupted by "what's the ETA?"

**Resolver:** The engineer(s) actually debugging and fixing the issue. They share findings in the incident channel but don't write external communications.

<Tip>
The communicator role is the most undervalued and most impactful. At Atlassian, incidents with a dedicated communicator resolved 40% faster — not because communication fixes bugs, but because it frees the resolver from context-switching to answer "are we fixed yet?" every 3 minutes.
</Tip>

## Communication Templates

Under stress, people write terrible updates. "We're looking into it" tells stakeholders nothing. Templates solve this.

### First status update (T+5 min)

```text
🔴 INCIDENT: [Brief description]
Severity: SEV[1-4]
Impact: [Who is affected and how]
Status: Investigating
Commander: @[name]

We detected [what happened] at [time]. We are currently investigating
the root cause. [Number] customers are affected. Next update in 25 minutes.
```

### Progress update (T+30 min, then hourly)

```text
🟡 UPDATE: [Brief description]
Status: [Investigating / Identified / Mitigating / Resolved]

What we know: [Current understanding of the issue]
What we're doing: [Specific mitigation steps in progress]
Impact: [Updated scope — better or worse than initial assessment?]
ETA: [Honest estimate, or "unknown — will update in 30 min"]
```

### Resolution update

```text
🟢 RESOLVED: [Brief description]
Duration: [Total time from detection to resolution]
Root cause: [One sentence]
Customer impact: [Who was affected, for how long]

A full postmortem will be published within 48 hours.
```

## War Room Etiquette

When multiple people are in an incident channel, things get chaotic fast. Rules I enforce:

1. **Prefix messages with your role.** `[RESOLVER] Checking database connection pool metrics` vs `[COMMS] Posting update to status page`. This prevents crosstalk.
2. **No drive-by debugging.** If you're not assigned as a resolver, don't start running queries or pushing fixes. Coordinate with the IC first.
3. **Thread long investigations.** The main channel should be a clean timeline. Deep dives go in threads.
4. **Say what you're doing before you do it.** "I'm going to restart the API pods" gives the IC a chance to say "wait, let me capture metrics first."
5. **Update regularly even when nothing has changed.** Silence during an incident is terrifying. "Still investigating, no new findings" is better than nothing for 20 minutes.

## The Postmortem That Prevents Repeats

Most postmortems are useless. They describe what happened, assign vague action items like "improve monitoring," and are never read again. Here's the structure that actually works:

```text
## Incident: [Title]
Date: [Date] | Duration: [Time] | Severity: SEV[X]
Author: [Name] | Reviewers: [Names]

## Summary
[2-3 sentences: what happened, who was affected, how it was resolved]

## Impact
- Users affected: [number]
- Revenue impact: [amount or "none"]
- Duration of customer impact: [time]

## Timeline (UTC)
- 02:47 — Alert: API error rate exceeded 5% threshold
- 02:49 — On-call acknowledged, opened incident channel
- 02:52 — Identified: connection pool exhaustion on payments-db
- 02:55 — First status update posted
- 03:01 — Mitigation: increased connection pool limit from 20 to 50
- 03:05 — Error rate dropping, monitoring
- 03:15 — Confirmed resolved, all metrics nominal

## Root Cause
[Detailed technical explanation. Not "the database was slow" but
"a query introduced in PR #1234 performed a full table scan on the
transactions table (4.2M rows) without an index on the created_at
column, exhausting the connection pool during peak hours."]

## What Went Well
- Alert fired within 2 minutes of impact starting
- On-call had relevant runbook for connection pool issues
- Mitigation was applied in under 15 minutes

## What Didn't Go Well
- The problematic query was not caught in code review
- No load testing was performed on the new endpoint
- Status page update was delayed by 10 minutes

## Action Items
| Action | Owner | Due Date | Ticket |
|--------|-------|----------|--------|
| Add index on transactions.created_at | @sarah | 2024-03-15 | ENG-1234 |
| Add query performance linting to CI | @avi | 2024-03-22 | ENG-1235 |
| Load test all new database queries | @team | 2024-04-01 | ENG-1236 |
```

<Info>
The action items section is the only part of the postmortem that matters long-term. Every action item needs an owner, a due date, and a ticket. "Improve monitoring" is not an action item. "Add alert for connection pool utilization above 80%, owned by @avi, due March 15, tracked in ENG-1234" is an action item.
</Info>

## Building an Incident Culture

The hardest part of incident response isn't the process — it's the culture. Blameless postmortems sound nice in theory. In practice, they require active enforcement:

- **Never ask "who broke this?"** Ask "what made this breakage possible?" The human who wrote the bug is the least interesting factor. The system that allowed it to reach production is the interesting factor.
- **Celebrate fast detection and clean response.** At Weel, we have a `#incident-wins` channel where we highlight incidents that were handled well. The message isn't "we had fewer incidents" — it's "we responded to this incident in 4 minutes and zero customers noticed."
- **Make incident participation career-positive.** If someone runs a great incident, mention it in their performance review. If on-call work is invisible, people will avoid it.
- **Review postmortem action items monthly.** Assign an engineering manager to track completion. Incomplete action items from postmortems are the number one way incidents repeat.

The goal isn't zero incidents — that's impossible at scale. The goal is that every incident makes the system more resilient, and no incident happens the same way twice.
