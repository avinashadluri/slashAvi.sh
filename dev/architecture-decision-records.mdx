Every technical disaster I've witnessed — and I've witnessed a few — started the same way: someone made a reasonable-sounding decision, didn't write down why, and six months later nobody could remember the constraints that led to it. The team either cargo-culted around a decision they didn't understand or reversed it without understanding the original trade-offs, stepping on the same landmine from a different angle.

Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) are the fix. Not because they're fancy or process-heavy — because they're a forcing function for clear thinking. Writing down "why" is harder than it sounds, and the act of doing it catches bad reasoning before it ships.

## Why Most Teams Skip ADRs (and Pay for It Later)

The objections are always the same: "We move too fast for documentation." "Everyone was in the meeting — we all know why." "We'll remember."

You won't. I've been on teams at Atlassian with 50+ engineers where a decision made in Q1 was being questioned in Q3 by people who joined in Q2. Without an ADR, the answer is always "I think it was because..." followed by an archaeology expedition through Slack messages and PR descriptions.

At Weel, we're a smaller team, and ADRs matter even more. When you're a startup with 10 engineers, every architectural decision carries disproportionate weight. Choose the wrong database, the wrong framework, the wrong deployment model — and you're living with it for years because you don't have the headcount to redo it.

<Info>
ADRs aren't about bureaucracy. A good ADR takes 30 minutes to write and saves dozens of hours of future debate. The best ones are under 500 words.
</Info>

## The Template I Use

I've tried several ADR formats — Michael Nygard's original, the Y-statement format, MADR. I've converged on this one because it's lightweight enough to actually get written:

```markdown
# ADR-{number}: {Title}

**Date:** YYYY-MM-DD
**Status:** Proposed | Accepted | Superseded by ADR-XXX | Deprecated
**Deciders:** {names}

## Context

What is the issue that we're seeing that is motivating this decision?
What are the forces at play (technical, organizational, time, cost)?
What constraints exist that narrow the solution space?

## Options Considered

### Option A: {Name}
- Pros: ...
- Cons: ...
- Estimated effort: ...

### Option B: {Name}
- Pros: ...
- Cons: ...
- Estimated effort: ...

### Option C: {Name} (if applicable)
- Pros: ...
- Cons: ...
- Estimated effort: ...

## Decision

We will go with **Option X** because {reasoning}.

## Consequences

### Positive
- ...

### Negative
- ...

### Risks
- ...

## Review Date

{Date by which we should revisit this decision}
```

The `Review Date` field is the most underrated part. It forces you to acknowledge that decisions have shelf lives. The technology landscape changes. Your team size changes. Your traffic patterns change. A decision that was correct 12 months ago might be wrong today, and that's fine — as long as you have a mechanism to revisit it.

## Real ADR Examples

### ADR: NestJS over Express for the Weel Backend

This was a real decision we made when rebuilding parts of the Weel backend.

| Criteria | Express | NestJS |
|----------|---------|--------|
| **Learning curve** | Low — team already knows it | Medium — decorators, DI, modules |
| **Structure at scale** | DIY — requires discipline | Opinionated — enforces consistency |
| **Testing** | Manual DI setup per test | Built-in testing module with DI |
| **Team size trajectory** | Works for 2-3 engineers | Scales to 10+ without architecture drift |
| **Ecosystem** | Massive, fragmented | Smaller, cohesive |
| **Cold start (serverless)** | Fast | Slower (DI container init) |

**Decision:** NestJS. Our backend was growing from 15 endpoints to 60+, and we were adding engineers. Express gave us freedom we were abusing — four different patterns for error handling, three for validation. NestJS's opinions would prevent that divergence.

**Consequence we accepted:** Slower cold starts ruled out serverless for some functions. We'd use long-running containers instead. Engineers needed 1-2 weeks to learn NestJS patterns. The trade-off was worth it for a team that was going to double in size.

### ADR: tRPC over GraphQL for Internal APIs

| Criteria | GraphQL | tRPC | REST |
|----------|---------|------|------|
| **Type safety** | Codegen required (extra build step) | End-to-end, zero codegen | Manual types or codegen |
| **Client complexity** | Apollo/urql, cache management | Simple function calls | fetch + types |
| **Schema evolution** | Versioning via deprecation | Breaking changes caught at compile time | URL versioning |
| **Public API suitability** | Excellent | Poor (TypeScript-only) | Excellent |
| **Team familiarity** | Moderate | Low (new to most) | High |

**Decision:** tRPC for internal APIs between our Next.js frontend and NestJS backend. REST for any external-facing APIs.

**The reasoning:** We're a TypeScript shop end-to-end. tRPC gives us type safety from database to UI with zero codegen, zero schema files, and zero build steps. GraphQL's value proposition — flexible queries, client-driven data fetching — matters for public APIs with diverse consumers. For an internal API where we control both sides, it's overhead without payoff.

<Warning>
tRPC locks you into TypeScript on both ends. If you ever need a mobile client in Swift/Kotlin or a third-party integration, you'll need a separate API layer. We accepted this because our near-term roadmap was web-only. Document assumptions like this — they're the first things that change.
</Warning>

## The Two-Way Door Framework

Jeff Bezos popularized this concept, and I use it constantly for technical decisions. Every decision falls into one of two categories:

**One-way doors** — Difficult or impossible to reverse. Examples: choosing a primary database, picking a programming language for a large codebase, committing to a cloud provider's proprietary services. These deserve ADRs, thorough analysis, and stakeholder buy-in.

**Two-way doors** — Easy to reverse with low cost. Examples: choosing a CSS-in-JS library, picking a testing framework, selecting a linting configuration. These should be made quickly by the person closest to the work.

The failure mode I see most often: teams treat two-way doors like one-way doors. They spend three weeks debating whether to use Jest or Vitest when the migration cost between them is a half-day of find-and-replace. Meanwhile, they treat one-way doors — like "let's use DynamoDB for everything" — as two-way doors and commit with a 30-minute Slack discussion.

```typescript
// A simple mental model I use
type Decision = {
  reversalCost: 'hours' | 'days' | 'weeks' | 'months';
  blastRadius: 'single-service' | 'multiple-services' | 'entire-platform';
  dataImpact: 'none' | 'migration-required' | 'data-loss-risk';
};

function needsADR(decision: Decision): boolean {
  return (
    decision.reversalCost === 'weeks' || decision.reversalCost === 'months' ||
    decision.blastRadius === 'entire-platform' ||
    decision.dataImpact === 'data-loss-risk'
  );
}
```

## How to Present Trade-offs to Leadership

Engineers often struggle to communicate technical decisions to non-technical stakeholders. The mistake is leading with technology names. Nobody above you in the org chart cares whether you use Kafka or SQS. They care about cost, timeline, risk, and capability.

My format for presenting to leadership:

1. **Problem statement in business terms.** "Our payment processing can't scale beyond 500 transactions/minute. We're at 380 and growing 15% monthly."
2. **Options with business impact.** Not "Option A: Kafka" but "Option A: 2 weeks to build, handles 10,000 tx/min, $800/month infrastructure cost."
3. **Recommendation with confidence level.** "I recommend Option B with high confidence. The risk is X, and we can mitigate it by Y."
4. **What you need from them.** "I need approval for 3 sprints of engineering time and a $500/month infrastructure budget increase."

<Tip>
Frame every technical decision in terms leadership cares about: time to deliver, cost, risk, and what it unlocks. Save the technical depth for the ADR document that engineers will reference.
</Tip>

## When to Revisit Decisions

An ADR isn't a tombstone — it's a living document. I revisit decisions when:

- **The context changed.** We assumed 100 requests/second; we're now at 1,000. The ADR was made for a different scale.
- **The team changed.** We assumed a team of 3 senior engineers; we now have 12 mixed-level. What was manageable is now chaotic.
- **The technology matured.** When we chose tRPC, it was v9 and unstable. Now it's v11 with a robust ecosystem. The "too risky" options from 2 years ago might be safe now.
- **We hit a predicted consequence.** Every ADR lists negative consequences. When those consequences actually materialize and become painful, it's time to reassess.

The review date field in the template is a trigger for this. When the date arrives, I scan the ADR and ask: "Are the assumptions still valid? Are the trade-offs still acceptable?" If yes, update the review date and move on. If no, write a new ADR that supersedes the old one.

## Making It Stick

ADRs only work if the team actually writes them. Here's how I've made them habitual:

1. **Store them in the repo**, not in Confluence or Notion. A `docs/adr/` directory that lives with the code. Searchable with `grep`. Reviewed in PRs.
2. **Make them part of the PR process.** Any PR that introduces a new dependency, changes infrastructure, or alters system architecture should include or reference an ADR.
3. **Keep them short.** If an ADR takes more than an hour to write, you're over-thinking it or the decision is more complex than you realized (which is itself useful information).
4. **Reference them in code comments.** When someone asks "why do we do it this way?" in code review, link to the ADR. Over time, the team internalizes that the answer exists in a known location.

The best engineering decisions aren't the ones where you pick the "right" technology. They're the ones where you understand the trade-offs, document them clearly, and give your future self the context to know when the decision should change.
