The best engineering mentor I ever had almost never told me what to do. She asked questions. Relentlessly good questions that made me realize I already knew the answer, or that I hadn't thought hard enough about the problem. It took me years to appreciate how much skill that required and how much restraint it demanded.

Now, as a staff engineer who spends a meaningful chunk of my time mentoring, I try to channel that approach. It's harder than it sounds. When you can see the answer clearly and the person across from you is struggling, the temptation to just tell them is almost irresistible. But every time you give someone the answer, you rob them of the learning that comes from finding it themselves.

Mentoring is not managing. It's not teaching. It's not pair programming. It's a specific kind of relationship where your job is to accelerate someone's growth by creating the right conditions — then getting out of the way.

## Mentoring vs Managing vs Teaching

These get conflated constantly, and the conflation leads to bad mentoring:

| | Mentoring | Managing | Teaching |
|-|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Goal** | Accelerate growth | Deliver outcomes | Transfer knowledge |
| **Power dynamic** | Peer-ish, voluntary | Hierarchical, formal | Expert-to-novice |
| **Primary tool** | Questions | Direction | Explanation |
| **Time horizon** | Long-term career | Quarter / sprint | Session / topic |
| **Success metric** | Mentee outgrows you | Team hits targets | Student understands concept |

As a staff engineer, I'm often in a fuzzy space where I'm technically not managing anyone but I have real influence over their work and career. That makes the distinction even more important. If I slip into managing mode, I undermine the mentee's autonomy. If I slip into teaching mode, I create dependency rather than growth.

<Info>
The best signal that you're mentoring well: the person starts bringing you harder problems. Not because they can't solve them, but because they want a thought partner. If they stop bringing you problems entirely, you either succeeded completely or they don't trust you enough. Figure out which one.
</Info>

## The Coaching Stance: Ask, Don't Tell

The single most impactful shift in my mentoring was learning to default to questions. Not leading questions that steer toward my preferred answer — genuine questions that help the other person think through the problem.

Instead of: "You should use a queue here."
Try: "What happens if this service goes down while a request is in flight?"

Instead of: "This API design won't scale."
Try: "How would this behave with 100x the current traffic? Walk me through the request path."

Instead of: "You need to break this into smaller PRs."
Try: "If you were reviewing this PR, where would you start? How long would it take?"

The question approach is slower in the moment and faster in the long run. The engineer who discovers through their own reasoning that they need a queue will internalize that pattern far more deeply than the one who was told to add one.

## Creating Stretch Assignments

Growth happens at the edge of comfort. Not in the panic zone, not in the cruise zone — right at the boundary where something is challenging but achievable with effort.

My framework for stretch assignments:

1. **Identify the growth edge** — what skill does this person need to develop next?
2. **Find a real problem that exercises that skill** — stretch assignments must be real work, not busywork
3. **Calibrate the challenge** — too easy and it's not growth; too hard and it's frustration
4. **Provide safety nets** — make it clear that failure is safe and you're available if they get stuck
5. **Debrief afterward** — the reflection is where the learning actually crystallizes

At Weel, I had a mid-level engineer who was technically strong but avoided cross-team work. I asked her to lead the API contract discussion between our team and the payments team. She was nervous. The discussion got heated at one point. But she navigated it, proposed a compromise, and got both teams aligned. That experience taught her more about staff-level work than any coding task could have.

<Tip>
The best stretch assignments are ones where the cost of failure is low but the learning value is high. Design doc reviews, internal tech talks, leading a spike, owning a small migration — these are all great growth vehicles that won't cause a production incident if things go sideways.
</Tip>

## 1:1 Frameworks That Work

I do mentoring 1:1s differently from managerial 1:1s. The mentee owns the agenda. My job is to listen, ask questions, and occasionally offer perspective from my own experience.

My structure:

- **First 5 minutes: check-in.** How are you doing? Not "what's your status" — genuinely, how are you doing. Context about someone's state of mind changes how I engage for the rest of the conversation.
- **Middle 20 minutes: their topics.** Whatever they want to discuss — technical problems, career questions, team dynamics, something they read, a decision they're wrestling with. I follow their lead.
- **Last 5 minutes: reflection.** What's one thing you're going to do differently this week? Not an assignment — a commitment they make to themselves.

I keep lightweight notes after each 1:1 — just the key themes and any commitments. This lets me notice patterns over time. If someone has mentioned feeling undervalued three times in a row, that's not a random topic — that's a signal.

## Giving Feedback That Lands

Bad feedback is vague ("you need to be more senior"), untimely (three months after the event), or delivered without context. Good feedback is specific, timely, and framed as observation + impact.

The formula I use:

**"When you [specific behavior], [specific impact]. What was your thinking there?"**

"When you merged that PR without addressing the API versioning comment, it meant the mobile team had to do a breaking change migration. What was your thinking there?"

This format does three things: it's specific enough to be actionable, it connects behavior to impact so the person understands why it matters, and it ends with a question that opens dialogue rather than shutting it down.

<Warning>
Feedback in public is almost always a mistake, even when it's positive. Public praise can embarrass introverts and create weird dynamics. Public criticism is obviously destructive. Default to private, always.
</Warning>

## Delegate the Problem, Not the Solution

This is the principle I come back to most often. When a mid-level engineer asks me how to solve something, my instinct is to hand them a solution. But the far better approach is to hand them the problem and let them find the solution.

"We need to reduce the p99 latency of the search endpoint from 800ms to under 200ms" is a problem. "Add a Redis cache in front of the database query with a 5-minute TTL" is a solution. The first framing gives the engineer space to investigate, learn, and potentially find a better approach than the one I had in mind.

At Atlassian, I delegated the problem of improving build times to a mid-level engineer. My mental solution was caching. She investigated and discovered that the real bottleneck was unnecessary test parallelization configuration, not missing caches. Her solution was simpler, cheaper, and better than mine would have been. That only happened because I gave her the problem, not my answer.

## The Mentoring Toolkit

Here's a practical reference for common mentoring situations:

| Situation | Approach | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| Mentee is stuck on a technical problem | Ask diagnostic questions, don't debug for them | "What have you tried? What did you expect vs what happened?" |
| Mentee is avoiding a hard conversation | Role-play the conversation, help them prepare | "Let's practice — I'll be the tech lead, pitch me your approach" |
| Mentee underestimates their work | Help them articulate impact in concrete terms | "Let's write down what changed because of what you built" |
| Mentee is frustrated with lack of promotion | Clarify expectations, identify gaps honestly | "Let's map your work against the staff rubric together" |
| Mentee is overcommitted | Help them prioritize and practice saying no | "If you could only do two of these five things, which two?" |
| Mentee disagrees with a team decision | Validate the disagreement, explore the context | "You might be right. What do you think the decision-maker was optimizing for?" |

## Recognizing Different Growth Paths

Not every engineer wants to be a staff engineer, and that's completely fine. Some engineers thrive going deep — becoming the world's foremost expert on a specific domain. Others thrive going broad — becoming the connective tissue between systems and teams. Both paths are valid, both are valuable, and forcing someone down the wrong path is a mentoring failure.

My job as a mentor is to help someone figure out which path energizes them, then help them walk it. If a brilliant systems engineer wants to go deep on database internals and never think about org strategy, my job is to help them become the best database engineer they can be — not to push them toward staff-level breadth they don't want.

## When to Push vs When to Support

Knowing when to push and when to support is the hardest judgment call in mentoring. Push too hard and you break trust. Support too much and you enable stagnation.

My heuristic: push when someone is avoiding growth out of fear, support when someone is processing a genuine setback. The difference is usually visible in how they talk about it. Fear sounds like "I don't think I'm ready" (push). Setback sounds like "I tried and it didn't work" (support).

At the end of the day, mentoring is an act of optimism. You're investing time and energy in someone's future capability. The returns are uncertain and delayed. But every senior engineer I've helped grow has made the teams around them better, and those teams have built better systems, and those systems have served users better. That's the compounding return of good mentoring — it doesn't stop with the person you're mentoring.
